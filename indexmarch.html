Fr. Kenneth Allen

On the Use of
Artificial Intelligence

The oldest vocation and the newest tool

Increase and multiply and fill the earth,
and make it yours

Genesis 1:28

That is the first commission given to the human race — before the fall, before the law, before the temple, before the covenant. Man is the one creature who does not simply inhabit the world — he names it. He orders it through dominion and stewardship; through cultivating and tending. Church teaching is unambiguous: we not only transform the world around us, but become more fully ourselves through work. Human work corresponds to human dignity, expresses it, and increases it.

The mandate to create is not incidental to human nature — it is constitutive of it. AI is not a shortcut. It is the latest extension of the oldest vocation.

You cannot learn the ocean from the shore. The hesitation most people feel is not prudence — it is the friction of the new, dressed up as wisdom.

Learn it. Use it. The tools of an age belong to those who pick them up, and every tool shapes the hand that holds it. The hammer teaches weight. The pen teaches precision. The algorithm, if we let it, teaches passivity — the quiet abdication of at least one great capacity that makes us irreplaceable: the capacity to mean something - both to ourselves, and to others. And that is precisely why, if for no other reason, you should pick AI up and master it.

Five Principles
I
Active

Use AI as a collaborator, not a replacement. Bring your question, your judgment, your dissatisfaction with the first answer. The machine generates; you discern. That distinction is not incidental — it is everything.

II
Conscious

Know what you are doing and why. Awareness of the tool's architecture — its training, its limits, its tendency toward the plausible over the true — is not a technical curiosity. It is a moral obligation.

III
Fully Engaged

Half-attention produces half-results at twice the risk. The danger of AI is not that it will think for you — it is that you will let it, and not notice until the thought that was supposed to be yours has gone missing.

IV
Ethical

Every output lands somewhere in the world and does something to someone. The question is not only can I generate this but what it will mean, in whose hands, for whose sake. The machine carries no conscience. You do.

V
Responsible

Own the output. Do not shelter behind the model's name when the words are yours to sign. Responsibility follows authorship, and authorship — the choice to publish, to send, to act — remains irreducibly human, whatever assisted the composition.
